For about two years, I got dressed the same way almost every day.

Black t-shirt, gray shorts. Or, in winter, black sweater, jeans. I own a closet full of clothes — some of it good stuff, stuff I actually like — and none of it made it into rotation. I'd just reach for the same thing, because it was easy, and working from home means nobody's really checking.

Buying more clothes didn't fix it. I tried that. A few new shirts would show up, get worn once, and sink back into the same drawer everything else lived in. The problem was never that I didn't own enough. The problem was that getting dressed had become a decision I didn't want to make, so I stopped making it.

---

Around the same time, I'd started building a handful of personal Claude skills — one for workouts, one for blog posts, one for weekly planning. The idea was simple: give Claude enough real context about my actual life that it stops giving generic advice and starts giving *my* advice.

So I built a wardrobe database. A Google Sheet — nearly 100 items — brand, color, fit, how often I actually wear it, and a notes field where things like "wear open over a white tee, never alone" live. Then, one day, I just told Claude I'd added a personal-style-coach skill and asked it to make something amazing with it. I didn't design what came back — I just handed over the data and let it figure out what to do with it.

Now when I ask for a week of outfits, it's not guessing. It's pulling from what I actually own, what's rated well, what pairs with what — and it puts the result straight onto my calendar as outfit events, so the decision's already made by the time I'm getting dressed. I'm not standing in front of the closet negotiating with myself at 7am anymore. I just look at what Tuesday says.

The wardrobe sheet didn't do anything on its own. I'd had it for months before this clicked. What changed was closing the loop — not just knowing what I own, but having something that actually tells me what to do with it, every day, without me having to think about it.

Last week I was in Madewell and two short-sleeve button-downs caught my eye — a solid black one and a green stripe, almost seersucker. The kind of thing I'd normally just grab because they looked fine on the hanger. Instead I took photos of both and asked Claude if they were actually worth it. The black one, it turned out, was a real gap — I had black tees, black sweaters, black polos, but not a single black short-sleeve button-down. The stripe was more of a nice-to-have, something to add texture to a rotation that was already full of solid overshirts. I bought both anyway. I'm wearing the stripe right now.

I still reach for the black t-shirt sometimes. But it's a choice now, not the only path of least resistance I had.
