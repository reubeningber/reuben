Last week I [wrote about](/articles/2026-03-12-ai-is-making-it-hard-to-step-away/) how AI was making it hard for me to step away from work. The always-on pull of a running agent, the anxiety that someone somewhere is prompting smarter and shipping faster. It resonated with a lot of people, which told me this isn't just a personal problem. It's a team problem.

And if it's a team problem, it's a management problem.

I've been watching my engineers navigate this shift in real time. The ones leaning hardest into AI tools are shipping faster than ever. That's genuinely exciting. But I've also noticed the other thing — later Slack messages, shorter lunch breaks, a low hum of anxiety that didn't used to be there. The same opportunity cost math I was doing at my own laptop, they're doing too.

Here's what makes this hard to manage: the pressure isn't coming from me. I'm not asking anyone to work longer hours. The pressure is coming from the tools themselves, and from the perceived gap between who's using them well and who isn't. As a manager, I can set expectations around hours. I can't as easily set expectations around a feeling.

So I've had to think differently about a few things.

**The culture I'm modeling matters more than ever.** If I'm sending messages at 10pm, my team notices — not because they think I expect a response, but because it quietly signals what "committed" looks like. AI makes it easy to keep going. That means the act of stopping has to become more deliberate, and more visible. I try to be vocal about logging off. About going for a run. About the fact that I came back sharper for it.

**How I evaluate performance is getting more complicated.** When someone ships three times as much as they did six months ago, is that excellence or is that someone running themselves into the ground? I don't think output alone tells me much anymore. I'm paying more attention to sustainability — are they making good decisions, or just fast ones? Are they learning, or just executing? AI can inflate the numbers while quietly hollowing out the work.

**The conversation has to be explicit.** I've started just asking directly: are you feeling pressure to stay online longer because of these tools? Most people won't raise it on their own. But when you ask, the answer is almost always yes. That opens the door to a real conversation about what we actually value as a team — and what we don't.

AI is the most powerful leverage I've seen come into engineering in my career. But leverage cuts both ways. My job as a manager isn't just to help my team use it well. It's to make sure they're still standing a year from now.
