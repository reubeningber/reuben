WHAT'S IN MY CAMERA BAG FOR 2026

What’s in My Camera Bag

I am absolutely a gear guy. I have always loved new toys.

I know this about myself. I research lenses for weeks before buying. I compare specs. I watch YouTube reviews. I have opinions about which third-party filters are worth it and which ones aren’t.

Some people think this is overthinking. I think it’s part of the fun.

My camera bag isn’t just about capturing my kids before they grow up—though that’s the main reason I carry it. It’s also about having the right tool for the job. About knowing my gear well enough that I’m not thinking about it when the moment happens.

Everything in this bag earned its spot. Some pieces solve specific problems. Others are there because they’re just really good at what they do.

Here’s what’s inside.

The Bag: Bellroy Camera Sling

I needed something I could grab on the way out the door. Not a backpack that requires planning. Not a shoulder bag that shifts around when I’m moving fast.

The sling works because it’s quick. Swings to the front when I need to swap lenses. Holds everything without announcing “photographer coming through.”

Could it have a water bottle pocket? Sure. But it’s fast, and fast matters when you’re trying to catch a moment before it’s gone.

The Bodies

Sony A6700

This is the upgrade I finally pulled the trigger on.

I shot with an A6000 for years, and it was legitimately great. But I started hitting its limits—autofocus that couldn’t track a running kid, unusable ISO above 1600, no flip screen for low angles.

The A6700 gave me:

  • Autofocus that actually keeps up
  • Clean files at ISO 6400 for indoor school events
  • A flip screen so I can shoot from floor level without contorting myself
  • Better ergonomics for all-day shooting

It’s not cheap. But I’m not buying another body for five years. This one needs to work through elementary school and whatever comes after.

Nikon L35AF

The film camera that just works.

Point-and-shoot. Sharp 35mm f/2.8 lens. Built like a tank. Completely automatic except for focus, which is delightfully manual.

I carry this because it changes how I shoot. With the Sony, I can fire off 50 shots and pick the best one later. With the L35AF, I get 36 frames. Maybe 24 if I’m being honest about what’s actually worth shooting.

It forces me to slow down. To be more intentional. To trust the moment instead of hoping I caught it.

And there’s something about film—the grain, the color rendering, the commitment of not knowing what you got until days later—that digital just doesn’t replicate.

The Lenses

Sony 35mm f/1.8

This lives on the camera 80% of the time.

It’s my lens for: breakfast table light, playground action, walking home from school, weekend mornings when everyone’s still in pajamas.

On a crop sensor, 35mm acts like a 52mm—close to how your eye actually sees. Which means the photos feel like memory, not performance.

Fast enough (f/1.8) to shoot indoors without cranking ISO. Small enough that the whole kit stays portable. Sharp enough that I’m never questioning whether the lens is holding me back.

If I could only keep one lens, this would be it. But I’m a gear guy, so that’s not happening.

Sigma 18-50mm f/2.8

The zoom that actually performs.

Most kit zooms are f/3.5-5.6, which makes them useless indoors. This stays at f/2.8 through the whole range—wide at 18mm, tight at 50mm, same amount of light.

I use this for:

  • Family gatherings where I can’t control distance
  • School events where I need flexibility
  • Travel days when I don’t want to swap lenses constantly

It’s not as magical as a prime. But it’s sharp, it’s fast, and it covers 90% of situations without making me think too hard.

Tamron 70-300mm

The reach lens.

I bought this specifically for dance recitals and future sports games. Situations where I’m stuck in an auditorium or bleachers, too far away for anything else to work.

It’s big. It’s heavy. It doesn’t live on the camera every day.

But when I need 200mm or 300mm of reach, nothing else does the job. And I’d rather carry the weight than crop a photo into oblivion later.

The Accessories

Shortstache Everyday Filter

A clear filter on whatever lens is mounted.

Does it improve image quality? No. Does it protect the front element from kid fingerprints, playground dirt, and general chaos? Yes.

I’ve already scratched one. Better than scratching a $400 lens.

Godox TT350 Flash

I resisted flash for a long time. Felt too aggressive, too staged.

Then I shot a winter concert in a dark auditorium and came home with nothing but noise and blur.

This flash is small, powerful enough to bounce off ceilings, and cheap enough that I don’t panic about breaking it. I dial it way down. I use it as fill, not main light.

But when I need it, I really need it.

Peak Design: Strap, Leash, Capture Clip

The Peak Design system solved three carrying problems:

The strap: Quick-release so I can pull the camera off my neck in half a second when I need both hands.

The leash: Thin wrist strap for when I want the camera ready but don’t need the full neck strap.

The Capture Clip: Clips to my belt or bag strap so the camera rides on my hip instead of swinging. Makes all-day carry actually comfortable.

Not essential. But they make carrying a camera all day feel less like work and more like second nature.

What’s Not in the Bag (Yet)

No massive f/1.4 primes. No ultrawide. No macro lens.

Not because I don’t want them—I’ve researched all of them. But because this kit covers what I actually shoot right now.

The 35mm handles daily life. The zoom covers unpredictability. The telephoto gives me reach. The film camera keeps me honest.

When I find a gap this setup can’t fill, I’ll add something. Until then, I’m shooting.

Why Gear Actually Matters

For a long time, the A6000 with a kit lens was enough. I got great photos. My kids didn’t care what I was shooting with.

But as they got older—faster, busier, further away—I started missing moments. The autofocus couldn’t keep up. The lens wasn’t fast enough. The files fell apart at higher ISOs.

So I upgraded. Not because I wanted better gear for its own sake. Because I wanted better photos of the thing I actually care about.

That’s the gear guy balance: Know your tools well enough that they disappear when it matters. Research enough to make smart choices. Then stop thinking and just shoot.

The bag. The bodies. The lenses. They’re all just tools.

But when you’ve got the right tools, you stop worrying about them and start noticing the light.

What’s in your camera bag? What did you research for months, and what did you impulse buy and somehow still love?
