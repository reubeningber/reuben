LEADERS DON’T TIPTOE — THEY JUMP

What The West Wing taught me about bold leadership.

Every leader faces a moment when they need to make a change that will shake things up — maybe a reorg, a new strategy, or a shift in priorities. It’s tempting to ease into it, to roll out the change step-by-step so people have time to “adjust.” But in my experience, incremental change doesn’t comfort people — it frustrates them. It breeds uncertainty, drags out discomfort, and leaves teams wondering whether their leaders actually know where they’re going.

A while back, I worked with a group of developers who had switched teams — or at least, that was the idea. In practice, they were still working on projects for their old teams while trying to start fresh with their new ones. They were straddling two worlds, answering to two different sets of expectations, and trying to make everyone happy. You can probably guess how that went.

No one was happy.

The developers were pulled in two directions. Their new teams couldn’t get them fully engaged. Their old teams felt abandoned. What should’ve been an exciting move — new projects, new opportunities — became a slow, awkward drift that took months to settle. We would’ve been far better off if we’d ripped the Band-Aid off. Either keep them where they were until their old projects wrapped up, or make the switch fully and let the new teams finish those projects themselves. But that middle ground — that half-measure — just bred discontent.

And that’s the thing about incremental change: it sends the message that leadership isn’t sure. It’s “wishy-washy.” It says, We’re thinking about making this change, but we’re not totally confident in it yet. And people feel that. When the direction feels uncertain, fear and frustration start to fill the gaps.

Decisive Leadership Builds Trust

Contrast that with a leader who makes a clear, confident move. They explain the “why,” set expectations, and move forward quickly. Even if not everyone agrees, the team senses strength. They might be nervous, but they’ll follow a leader who seems sure of the path.

It’s like when you’re hiking with your kids. You come to a fork in the trail — left or right? The kids don’t care which way you go; they just want to know you know. If you stand there second-guessing yourself, they’ll start to lose faith. But if you say, “We’re going right. Let’s go!” they’ll fall in line, even if you have to double back later. Confidence isn’t about being infallible — it’s about showing that you’re committed to the direction you’ve chosen.

The West Wing and the Leap of Leadership

There’s a scene in The West Wing that captures this perfectly. President Bartlet asks C.J. Cregg to become his Chief of Staff — a huge change for her and for the administration. She’s hesitant, understandably; it’s a big leap. Bartlet looks her in the eye and ask, “I need you to jump off cliff?”

That’s leadership. It’s not about easing people into the unknown one inch at a time — it’s about inviting them to take the leap with you. Bartlet didn’t promise it would be smooth or easy. He just made it clear he believed in the decision and was jumping too. That’s what real leadership feels like: not distant or cautious, but shoulder-to-shoulder in the risk.

The Business Case for Boldness

You can see this same principle in business. When Satya Nadella took over as CEO of Microsoft in 2014, he made swift, decisive changes. He scrapped the old “know-it-all” culture and replaced it with one centered on learning and collaboration. He wasn’t timid about it. He talked about empathy, purpose, and growth mindset from day one — and backed it up with action. Layoffs, reorganizations, product shifts — they all happened quickly, with clear communication about why.

The result? Microsoft’s culture transformed. Employees didn’t rebel against the pace; they rallied around the clarity. They could see that Nadella wasn’t guessing his way through change — he was committed. Swift action, grounded in purpose, built credibility and momentum.

Leading with Confidence and Care

Now, ripping the Band-Aid doesn’t mean ignoring empathy. Swift doesn’t have to mean harsh. In fact, the most effective leaders communicate more — not less — when they move quickly.

You explain why the change is happening. You make it clear that you’re standing beside your team, not above them. You say, “Yes, this might be bumpy, but I’m here for the bumps.”

That’s the difference between pushing people off a cliff and jumping together.

Confidence Breeds Strength

At the end of the day, leadership is about belief — belief in your vision, belief in your team, and belief in your ability to handle whatever comes next. Incrementalism, for all its good intentions, too often signals fear. And fear is contagious.

But so is confidence.

When you move with clarity and conviction, people feel it. They may not love the change, but they’ll trust it. They’ll trust you.

Because when it comes to change, teams don’t need perfection — they need a leader who’s willing to leap first.
